# Coach V4 User Mental Model

> **Purpose:** Explain what the Coach *is*, as a user would understand it — not how it is built.  
> **Audience:** Product, design, onboarding, marketing.

---

## What the Coach is

The Coach is not a dashboard, a plan viewer, or a notification feed. It is a **single opinion** about the most useful thing to pay attention to **right now**.

It answers one question at a time:

> *"Given how I am today and what's ahead, what is the one thing that would help most — or do I need to do nothing?"*

The Coach appears in two places:

| Surface | What it feels like |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Today card** | A quick nudge — headline + one action. Tactical when you're mid-workout or about to start. |
| **Coach tab** | The reasoning — why this matters, what to do, what to avoid. More context, less urgency. |

They intentionally say **different things** during live workouts: Today tells you how to pace; Coach tells you why holding back is enough.

---

## The ten jobs the Coach actually does

Users don't need 59 scenarios. They need **ten jobs** the Coach performs — only one leads at a time.

### 1. Guide an active workout

**When:** You're in a session right now.

**What it sounds like:**  
*"Don't chase the numbers." · "Ease up now." · "Keep the walk easy."*

**What it's really saying:**  
Finish usefully. Don't turn this block into extra stress. Adjust effort before fatigue decides for you.

**You notice it when:** The badge feels "live" and the advice is about pace, reserve, or moderation — not food, not tomorrow.

---

### 2. Prepare you for a workout

**When:** Something meaningful is coming — soon or later today.

**What it sounds like:**  
*"Prepare for the start." · "Set up the ride properly." · "Eat lightly and hydrate before you go."*

**What it's really saying:**  
Show up fed, hydrated, and calm. The session is won in the hour before it, not in the first hard interval.

**You notice it when:** A session is on the horizon and the Coach talks about arriving ready, not executing yet.

---

### 3. Help you recover after work

**When:** You just finished something that mattered.

**What it sounds like:**  
*"Recovery leads now." · "Protect the work you just did." · "Refuel and rehydrate, then keep the rest of the day easy."*

**What it's really saying:**  
The training is banked. Absorption — food, fluids, calm — is what makes it count. Don't stack more load.

**You notice it when:** Hard work is behind you and the Coach stops talking about performance.

---

### 4. Tell you the day is fine

**When:** Nothing is wrong. No urgent lever.

**What it sounds like:**  
*"Nothing needs fixing." · "No changes needed." · "Keep moving at your usual rhythm."*

**What it's really saying:**  
You don't need to optimize, compensate, or fix anything. Stay steady.

**You notice it when:** Green, calm copy with no specific call to eat, drink, push, or pull back. **This should be the most common voice.**

---

### 5. Pull you back when the plan is too ambitious

**When:** Sleep, recovery, or load say today's plan asks too much.

**What it sounds like:**  
*"Manage intensity." · "Readiness sets the ceiling." · "Keep the run, lower the ceiling."*

**What it's really saying:**  
The workout can still happen — but not at full plan intensity. Let the warm-up decide. Shorten if needed.

**You notice it when:** You planned hard work but the Coach explicitly negotiates effort down, not just "start easy."

---

### 6. Protect tomorrow

**When:** Something important is scheduled tomorrow and tonight's choices matter.

**What it sounds like:**  
*"Protect tomorrow." · "Tonight sets up tomorrow." · "Wind down — no extra load tonight."*

**What it's really saying:**  
What you spend tonight costs tomorrow's session. Recovery starts now, not in the morning.

**You notice it when:** Evening tone + explicit reference to what's coming tomorrow (or implied by "protect tomorrow").

---

### 7. Remind you to drink

**When:** Fluids are low and timing makes it consequential — heat, training, afternoon, post-sweat.

**What it sounds like:**  
*"Fluids need attention." · "Sip steadily through the rest of the day." · "Water comes first before heat."*

**What it's really saying:**  
You're behind on something cheap to fix. Sip now so the next decision isn't reactive.

**You notice it when:** Water is the main verb — not bundled silently inside prep unless critical.

---

### 8. Remind you to eat

**When:** Energy is low relative to what's being asked — especially before training.

**What it sounds like:**  
*"Energy needs a top-up." · "Eat before you ask for more effort." · "A little food would help right now."*

**What it's really saying:**  
You're asking for output without input. A small amount of food now prevents a bad session later.

**You notice it when:** Food is the main verb, or prep explicitly says "eat a little before you go."

---

### 9. Guide heat and sauna

**When:** Sauna or heat exposure is planned, active, or just finished.

**What it sounds like:**  
*"Top up water before heat." · "Keep the heat moderate." · "Sauna is recovery heat, not training."*

**What it's really saying:**  
Heat is recovery, not a workout. Hydrate first, stay moderate, leave before fatigue, rest after.

**You notice it when:** Sauna/heat is named and the Coach never talks about it like a training session.

---

### 10. Shape recovery days and tired days

**When:** The plan says recovery, or your body clearly needs a quieter day.

**What it sounds like:**  
*"Recovery day · Keep movement easy." · "Sleep leads today." · "Protect recovery · The body needs a calmer day."*

**What it's really saying:**  
Today's win is finishing fresher than you started — not proving fitness. Rest is the intervention.

**You notice it when:** Whole-day posture, not session-specific. Often purple/recovery tone.

## What the Coach does *not* do

Understanding the mental model also means knowing the boundaries:

- **It is not a calendar.** It won't countdown "session in 47 min" on the Today card — that's schedule, not coaching.
- **It is not a nutrition tracker.** It mentions food and water when they change the next decision — not to hit daily targets.
- **It is not a workout coach in the gym-sense.** During live sessions it advises restraint and reserve, not sets, reps, or zones.
- **It is not guilt-driven.** Missed workouts get "reset the day," not "make it up."
- **It is not always on.** Quiet days are a feature — *"nothing needs fixing"* is a successful outcome.

## How the ten jobs map to a day

```
Morning          →  Start calm (job 4, 10) or manage readiness (job 5)
                 →  Prepare for later session (job 2, 7, 8)

Midday           →  All clear (job 4) or drink/eat (jobs 7, 8)
                 →  Sequence multiple events (job 2 + 9)

Before session   →  Prepare (job 2, 7, 8) or dial back (job 5)

During session   →  Guide active workout (job 1) or guide heat (job 9)

After session    →  Recover (job 3)

Evening          →  Close out (job 4) or protect tomorrow (job 6)
                 →  Recover after late hard work (job 3 + 6)
```

Only **one job leads** at a time. The Coach never asks you to push and protect simultaneously, or to prep and recover at once.

---

## Priority order (what wins the user's attention)

When multiple things are true, the Coach roughly follows this hierarchy — higher items replace lower ones:

1. **You're in a live session** → guide the workout (or heat)
2. **You just finished hard work** → recover
3. **Session starts very soon** → prepare (or dial back if readiness is poor)
4. **Body is clearly depleted** → recovery-led day
5. **Tomorrow needs protection** → protect tomorrow (especially evening)
6. **Fluids or food block the next move** → hydrate or fuel
7. **Plan is too ambitious for today's readiness** → dial back
8. **Nothing urgent** → all clear

Users don't see this list. They just see one story.

## Emotional register by job

| Job | User should feel… | Should NOT feel… |
|-----|-------------------|------------------|
| Active workout | Supported, in control | Micromanaged, scolded |
| Preparation | Ready, unhurried | Anxious, behind schedule |
| Post-workout recovery | Accomplished, guided | Lazy, guilty for stopping |
| All clear | Calm, trusted | Ignored, bored |
| Dial back | Respected, adaptive | Weak, failing the plan |
| Protect tomorrow | Strategic, long-term | Restricted for no reason |
| Hydrate / Fuel | Reminded, not nagged | Shamed about tracking gaps |
| Heat / Sauna | Safe, informed | Like it's a workout PR |
| Recovery / tired day | Permitted to rest | Like they're broken |

## The simplified promise

If we described the Coach in one sentence to a new user:

> **"WeekFit's Coach watches your recovery, your plan, and your day — and tells you the one thing worth doing right now, or tells you to relax because nothing needs fixing."**

That's the whole product. Everything else is tone variation within ten jobs.

---

## Recommended product north star

Based on this audit, the Coach should optimize for:

1. **All Clear as default** — most days, users hear calm confirmation, not intervention.
2. **One leading job** — never blend "push harder" and "protect tomorrow" in the same moment.
3. **Today = action, Coach = meaning** — especially during workouts.
4. **Name the stake** — "protect tomorrow's long run" beats "protect tomorrow."
5. **Fewer green-light variants** — merge "good window," "stable morning," and "no changes needed" into one calm family users recognize.
